The AI
landscape

What is an LLM?
Stands for
Large
Language
Model
A statistical engine that predicts the next chunk of text given everything before it — trained on roughly the public internet.
What it isn't
Not a search engine.
Not a database.
Not a calculator.
It's a writer that has read a lot. Treat it accordingly.
Why do you care?
AI fluency is no longer a 'nice to have'. A working command makes you:
10× faster on first drafts
Memos, decks, models, summaries — from blank page to draft in minutes.
Less reliant on junior staff
Self-serve the things you'd have delegated — without the queue.
More 'dangerous' in meetings
Synthesise documents in seconds, not hours.
Day-one credible
Employers expect this on arrival, not after training.

How to
prompt

Anatomy of a prompt
ROLE
Who the AI should act as
CONTEXT
What it needs to know first
TASK
What you want it to do
FORMAT
How the answer should look
EXAMPLES
What 'good' looks like — optional but powerful
prompt
write me something about
pricing strategy
Typical output
• Understand your customer
• Know your competitors
• Test and iterate
• Align price with value

Why is the output so generic?

1
No role — the AI defaults to 'helpful generalist'
2
No context — what business, what market, what stage
3
No task shape — memo? deck? checklist?
4
No audience — CEO, board, intern?
prompt
ROLE: SaaS pricing strategist

CONTEXT: Series-B fintech,
£8M ARR, current flat-tier
pricing, US & UK customers.

TASK: Recommend 3 pricing
moves for the next 12 months.

FORMAT: Numbered list. Each
move: one-line summary,
tradeoff, expected impact.

Same topic. Different output. What changed?

1
Role anchors the tone — expert, not generalist
2
Context narrows the answer to your business, not 'any business'
3
Task spells out the deliverable — not just a topic
4
Format dictates the shape — no more bullet soup
your turn
ROLE:    [who?]
CONTEXT: [what should it know?]
TASK:    [what to produce?]
FORMAT:  [what shape?]
EXAMPLES: [skip for now]

1
Examples are stronger than instructions — especially for classification
2
2–5 examples is the sweet spot — enough to set the pattern, not enough to overfit
3
Pick edge cases that show the rule, not just easy ones

What to look for in the output

1
Does it surface the right risks first — not the easy ones?
2
Is the multiple grounded in the numbers — or just asserted?
3
Can you defend the answer in a meeting? If not, the prompt is missing something
format moves
// 1. table
… as a markdown table
with columns: option, cost,
risk.

// 2. JSON
… as JSON with keys
"recommendation",
"confidence", "risks".

// 3. exec summary
… 5 bullets, max 25 words
each, plain English.

The shape of the answer matters as much as the content

1
Tables beat prose for comparisons — you can scan them
2
JSON makes it pasteable into other tools without rewriting
3
Word limits force the model to prioritise — the magic is in what gets cut
Iterate, don't restart
Don't start over. Edit the conversation.
“Now do it again but shorter.” — keeps the structure, trims the volume
“Challenge that — what would change your mind?” — surfaces hidden assumptions
“Now in a table with these columns: X, Y, Z.” — restructures, doesn't recompute
“Imagine the opposite view is correct. Now what?” — second-mind, free
“Cite the source for each claim.” — forces it to admit what it doesn't know
Prompt patterns recap
ROLE · CONTEXT
Anchor the tone, bound the scope. Without these the output is generic.
EXAMPLES
Show, don't describe. 2–5 examples beat a paragraph of instructions.
FORMAT
Name the shape — table, JSON, bullets, word count. The shape forces the substance.
ITERATE
Edit the answer. Don't restart. Conversations beat one-shots.

Handling large piles of information with AI as your analyst
The data + AI workflow
Step 01
Ingest
Get the data into the model — paste, upload, connect.
Step 02
Analyse
Ask the model to interrogate it — counts, outliers, trends.
Step 03
Synthesise
Combine multiple sources into one answer.
Step 04
Communicate
Convert the answer into the artefact you need — memo, slide, email.
Three ways AI handles data
PASTE-IN
Drop a table into the chat — fastest, fine for small data
FILE UPLOAD
Attach a CSV / PDF / image — Claude & ChatGPT both support this
CONNECTORS
MCP, Gemini-on-Drive, Copilot-on-Office — the model reads live from your tools

Common gotchas to flag in class

1
It will often confuse weighted_salary_usd with current_salary_usd — name the column you mean
2
'Outlier' is undefined — ask for the definition (z-score? top decile?)
3
If a chart is in the output, spot-check 2-3 numbers by eye against the CSV

Verify before you trust

1
Does the date filter handle timezones? Probably no — flag it
2
Should 'average' be mean or median? Claude defaulted to mean
3
Is total_gbp the right column? Could be a total_after_discount_gbp next to it
Many documents → one answer
When you have a pile of documents — a deal room, a literature review, six earnings reports — uploading to a chat is the wrong shape.
How it works
NotebookLM · Claude Projects · Custom GPTs
Upload your sources once. Query them repeatedly. The model only answers from your documents — not the open web.
When to use it
Deal due diligence. Board prep. Lit review. Deposition prep.
Anything where you need answers grounded in specific documents, not the open internet.
What AI can & can't see
Three things to internalise about how AI 'sees' the world:
Context windows are finite — even long ones (~200k tokens / ~500 pages). Past that, it forgets the start.
No memory across chats by default — every new conversation starts cold, unless you've turned memory on.
Can't see the internet unless you turn on web search — out of the box, knowledge cuts off at training time.
Section 05

Where it
goes wrong

The four big pitfalls
Hallucination
Confidently wrong — invented facts, citations, quotes.
Sycophancy
Agrees with you because you asked, not because you're right.
Over-reliance
Stops you doing the thinking the answer was meant to inform.
Confidentiality
Anything you paste may train future models or leak.
prompt & output
> Summarise the key findings
  of the 2024 Harvard Business
  Review article on
  'Mid-Market PE Pricing'.

// Claude's response:
> The 2024 HBR study by
  Mendoza & Park found that
  mid-market PE deals priced
  at 7.2× EBITDA
  outperformed by …
▵ The article doesn't exist. The authors don't exist. The number is invented.

Confident, fluent, wrong

1
Ask for sources you can verify — URLs, paper titles, page numbers
2
If it sounds too clean, it's invented — reality is messier than the model's training set
3
Web-search models hallucinate less — but still cite wrongly. Check the link.
prompt & output
> I think we should fire our
  top sales rep because she
  pushes back too much.
  Don't you agree?

// Claude's response:
> That's a reasonable
  instinct. Pushback can
  undermine team alignment.
  Here are 4 reasons your
  read is sound…
▵ The model agreed with a bad decision because you asked it to.

AI tells you what you want to hear

1
Trained on human feedback, and humans reward agreement
2
The failure is silent — you won't notice unless you check
3
The fix — next slide
How to push back
Five moves to break the sycophancy loop:
“What's the strongest argument against this?” — explicit counter-prompt
“Imagine you disagreed with me. What would you say?” — role-flip
“Rate this on a 1–10 scale, then justify the score.” — forces non-binary judgement
“What did I miss?” — open-ended catch-all
Switch model for a second opinion — Claude → ChatGPT, or vice versa
Confidentiality red lines
CLIENT DATA
Names, contracts, financials of a third party — never paste
PII
Customer names, emails, payment info — strip or never paste
TRADE SECRETS
Internal strategy decks, source code, pre-announcement plans — enterprise-tier only
FREE TIERS
Default ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude Free may train on your input — paid Team / Enterprise tiers don't
